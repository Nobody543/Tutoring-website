About the TMUA
The Test of Mathematics for University Admission (TMUA) is a two-and-a-half-hour, multiple-choice admissions test used by a number of leading UK universities — including Oxford, Cambridge, Imperial, LSE, UCL, Durham, and Warwick — as part of their selection process for Mathematics, Computer Science, Economics, and related courses. It consists of two papers: Paper 1 tests the application of mathematical knowledge, and Paper 2 assesses mathematical reasoning and proof. Scores run from 1.0 to 9.0. Oxford now uses the TMUA under new guidance, having moved away from its previous MAT. Crucially, the TMUA is not a knowledge test — it tests how you think mathematically, which is what makes preparation genuinely valuable.
This is one of the most important things to understand, and one of the most common sources of underperformance. A-Level Maths rewards structured, method-driven solutions — you learn the technique, apply it, and get the marks. TMUA rewards something different: the ability to reason quickly under pressure about unfamiliar mathematical situations, often with no obvious method to reach for. Students who are excellent at A-Level frequently find TMUA harder than expected, because the instincts A-Level builds — find the right formula, work through the steps — can actively work against you. Developing the right kind of thinking is exactly what my preparation method is designed to do.
TMUA scores above 6.5 are considered strong, and scores above 7.5 are genuinely competitive for Oxford and Cambridge. The national average tends to sit around 5.0–5.5. I scored 8.5. What's realistic for you depends on your starting point and the work you put in — but structured preparation consistently makes a significant difference. The students who perform below expectations are almost always those who prepared using A-Level instincts alone, without understanding what the test actually demands. That's precisely the gap I work to close.
